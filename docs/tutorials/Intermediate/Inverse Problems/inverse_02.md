# Inverse Problems: Hyperelastic Example

## Problem Description

We consider recovery of a material parameter from displacement observations in a compressible hyperelastic solid. The forward problem is quasi-static equilibrium under a fixed Neumann traction; the parameter is a scalar $m \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ that enters the constitutive law but not the boundary conditions.

**Domain.** The reference configuration is the unit cube $\Omega = [0,1]^3$, meshed with $10 \times 10 \times 10$ first-order hexahedra.

**Boundary conditions.** The left face $\Gamma_D = \{x_1 = 0\}$ is fully clamped ($u = 0$ on $\Gamma_D$). A uniform compressive traction $\bar{\mathbf{t}} = (-10, 0, 0)$ is applied on the right face $\Gamma_N = \{x_1 = 1\}$.

## Material Model

The strain energy density is

$$
\Psi(\mathbf{F}; m) = \frac{\mu(m)}{2}\left(J^{-2/3} I_1 - 3\right) + \frac{\kappa(m)}{2}(J - 1)^2
$$

where $J = \det \mathbf{F}$, $I_1 = \mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{F}^T \mathbf{F})$, and

$$
\mu(m) = \frac{m}{2(1 + \nu)}, \qquad \kappa(m) = \frac{m}{3(1 - 2\nu)}, \qquad \nu = 0.45.
$$

The $J^{-2/3}$ prefactor on $I_1$ enforces a volumetric-isochoric split, making the deviatoric response independent of bulk compression. The first Piola-Kirchhoff stress $\mathbf{P} = \partial \Psi / \partial \mathbf{F}$ is obtained by differentiating through the energy expression.

**Note:** This is a distinct strain energy from the standard Neo-Hookean form $\Psi = \frac{\mu}{2}(I_1 - 3 - 2\ln J) + \frac{\kappa}{2}(J-1)^2$ used in the introductory tutorials. The two coincide in the incompressible limit but differ at finite compressibility.

## Forward Problem

The weak form of equilibrium is: find $u \in \mathcal{U}$ satisfying the Dirichlet condition on $\Gamma_D$ such that

$$
\int_\Omega \mathbf{P}(\nabla u; m) : \nabla v \, dV = \int_{\Gamma_N} \bar{\mathbf{t}} \cdot v \, dS \qquad \forall v \in \mathcal{V}.
$$

After assembly this is the nonlinear system $\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{m}) = \mathbf{0}$, solved by Newton's method.

**Parameter representation.** In this example, $m$ is a single scalar broadcast uniformly across all mesh nodes, so the inverse problem is a $\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ optimization problem. It is passed to `set_params` as an array of shape `(n_nodes, 1)` with a constant value, and mapped to quadrature-point values via `convert_dof_to_quad` before residual assembly.

## Inverse Problem Setup

Given displacement observations $u^{\mathrm{obs}} \in \mathcal{U}^h$, the objective is the $\ell^2$ mismatch:

$$
J(u) = \|u - u^{\mathrm{obs}}\|_2
$$

and the inverse problem is $\min_{m} J(u(m))$ subject to the equilibrium constraint.

### Synthetic Data

Ground truth data is generated by solving the forward problem at $m^* = 80$. The inverse solve is initialized at $m_0 = 50$. This is an "inverse crime" setup — model, mesh, and observation operator are identical in the forward and inverse passes — which isolates the optimization machinery from questions of measurement noise and model error.

### Optimizer

Since this is a $\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ problem, the outer loop uses a simple gradient descent with a step size derived from the loss-to-gradient ratio:

$$
m_{k+1} = m_k - \alpha \frac{J_k}{\nabla J_k}
$$

where $\alpha = 0.25$ is a fixed damping factor. A box constraint $m \in [50, 100]$ is enforced by clipping at each step. Future versions of CARDIAX will include L-BFGS for higher-dimensional parameter estimation.
